A father's daily reminder

Father Forgets

On a poem by W. Livingston Larned, first published 1927 — and why I return to it.

Essay Familie Lyrik

There is a poem I try to read every day. I will be honest: some days I manage it, some days life gets in the way. But even on the days I don't read it, it reads me.

It is the confession of a father standing at his sleeping son's bedside, suddenly seeing the whole day again — the scolding at breakfast, the sharp words over small things, the frown where a wave goodbye deserved a smile. And in the quiet of that room, he understands what he had forgotten all day long: he had been measuring his boy by the yardstick of his own adult years.

“I was expecting too much of youth.” — W. Livingston Larned, Father Forgets (1927)

That line is the whole reason this page exists. As a father, I know exactly how it happens. Children spill things. They dawdle. They do precisely the thing you asked them not to do, thirty seconds after you asked. And anger comes so easily — faster than thought, faster than love.

But they are just children. That is not an excuse for them; it is a duty for us. They are small, and fragile, and they are learning the world from scratch, and the person they learn it from most is you. When they make you angry, that is exactly the moment to remember what they are: not small adults failing at adulthood, but children succeeding at childhood — messily, loudly, beautifully.

You care for them. You hold them. And one day, far sooner than you think, they will not be small anymore. Reading this poem — every day, or as often as I honestly manage — makes me a better father. Not a perfect one. A better one. That is enough to start each morning with.
